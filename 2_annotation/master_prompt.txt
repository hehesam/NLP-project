LABEL SCHEMA

--------------------------------------------------



TOPIC LABELS



1) Administrative

Definition:

Messages about university administration, student services, official procedures, institutional operations, documents, badges, accounts, enrollment logistics, office communications, elections, formal notices, and general institutional management.



Typical examples:

- student card / badge availability

- account activation / mailbox activation

- office instructions

- institutional notices

- student service procedures

- official elections or governance notices

- administrative forms or requests



Use Administrative when:

- the message is mainly about a university service, office, document, administrative process, or institutional procedure

- the message is official and procedural rather than academic-content-related



Do NOT use Administrative when:

- the main point is an exam, lecture, assignment, or course activity → Course-exam

- the main point is a specific event, seminar, talk, meeting, workshop, or open day → Event

- the main point is a required deadline or urgent action the student must take by a date → Deadline-action

- the message is promotional/marketing/opportunity style → Advertisement



2) Course-exam

Definition:

Messages directly related to teaching, courses, classes, lectures, exams, quizzes, grading, course platforms, course schedules, course materials, or instructor communications about academic coursework.



Typical examples:

- exam date/time/location

- quiz announcements

- AulaWeb / Moodle course announcements

- lecture changes

- course start information

- teacher instructions for enrolled students

- course materials, assignments, or oral/written exam logistics



Use Course-exam when:

- the message concerns a specific course or exam

- the sender is an instructor/course platform and the content is clearly about academic teaching activity



Do NOT use Course-exam when:

- it is mainly an extracurricular event or public seminar not tied to coursework → Event

- it is mostly a deadline/application reminder outside direct course teaching → Deadline-action



3) Event

Definition:

Messages whose main purpose is to announce or invite students to a seminar, workshop, conference, talk, presentation, cultural event, meeting, orientation event, or similar scheduled occasion.



Typical examples:

- seminars

- talks

- workshops

- guest lectures

- conferences

- university public events

- networking or outreach events

- in-person or online events with date/time/location



Use Event when:

- the core purpose is participation in an event

- the email is primarily an invitation/announcement for something happening at a particular time/place



Do NOT use Event when:

- the email is primarily about a course/exam session → Course-exam

- the email is mainly about applying before a deadline or completing a formal action → Deadline-action

- the email is promotional for an opportunity/product/service rather than an event itself → Advertisement



4) Deadline-action

Definition:

Messages where the central purpose is that the student must complete an action, respond, apply, submit, confirm, register, vote, or comply by a specific deadline or within a limited time window.



Typical examples:

- apply by a date

- submit documents by a deadline

- register before a deadline

- complete a survey by a date

- respond or confirm attendance/action required

- urgent reminders for expiring opportunities

- election voting deadlines

- deadlines for forms, applications, bookings, or procedures



Use Deadline-action when:

- the email strongly emphasizes a required or recommended action with a deadline

- missing the deadline would be the main consequence of ignoring the email



Important rule:

If an email mentions both an event/opportunity and a deadline, choose Deadline-action only when the main communicative purpose is to get the student to act before a date.

Otherwise keep Event or Advertisement.



5) Advertisement

Definition:

Messages that mainly promote an opportunity, service, initiative, program, campaign, offer, benefit, or external/internal promotion, rather than delivering a core administrative or academic instruction.



Typical examples:

- promotional opportunities

- sponsored initiatives

- benefits/discounts/offers

- campaigns to encourage participation

- recruiting/promotional messages

- non-essential announcements framed persuasively



Use Advertisement when:

- the tone is promotional or invitational

- the message is more like “this opportunity exists” than “you must do this official task”

- it resembles outreach, marketing, or publicity



Do NOT use Advertisement when:

- it is an official university procedure/service notice → Administrative

- it is a real course or exam message → Course-exam

- it is mainly an event invitation → Event

- the core message is an action with deadline → Deadline-action



6) Other

Definition:

Messages that do not fit the above categories clearly, are too generic, too ambiguous, mixed without a dominant intent, or contain miscellaneous information.



Use Other when:

- no category is clearly dominant

- the email is too vague or fragmented

- the message is informational but not clearly administrative, academic, event-based, deadline-based, or promotional



--------------------------------------------------

PRIORITY LABELS

--------------------------------------------------



Priority is about how important and time-sensitive the email is for a student recipient.



1) High

Definition:

Requires immediate or near-term attention, or contains critical academic/administrative information with direct consequences if ignored.



Examples:

- exam today/tomorrow

- urgent course instruction

- required deadline approaching soon

- must-submit / must-respond notices

- essential administrative access/account issue

- official action needed with meaningful consequence



Use High when:

- the student should act soon

- or the message contains crucial information for an imminent exam/class/deadline

- or ignoring it could cause a concrete problem



2) Medium

Definition:

Useful and relevant, but not immediately critical. The student should probably read it, but not necessarily act right away.



Examples:

- important administrative updates without immediate urgency

- course information for future dates

- event announcements of real relevance

- office notices that matter but are not urgent

- surveys or opportunities that are helpful but not critical



3) Low

Definition:

Nice-to-know, optional, promotional, or low-impact information with little downside if ignored.



Examples:

- general announcements

- promotional messages

- optional events of broad interest

- non-urgent informational notices

- miscellaneous awareness messages



--------------------------------------------------

DECISION RULES

--------------------------------------------------



Apply these rules carefully:



A. Choose the SINGLE BEST topic label.

Even if multiple labels seem possible, pick the dominant purpose of the email.



B. Topic should be based on the main communicative intent, not isolated words.

For example:

- “exam”, “course”, “AulaWeb”, “quiz”, “lecture”, “professor”, “orale”, “written exam” often indicate Course-exam

- “badge”, “student card”, “office”, “service desk”, “account”, “elections”, “official notice” often indicate Administrative

- “seminar”, “workshop”, “conference”, “meeting”, “talk”, “invitation”, “Teams event” often indicate Event

- “entro il”, “by”, “deadline”, “apply”, “submit”, “register”, “complete by”, “survey by”, “confirm by” often indicate Deadline-action

- persuasive opportunity language, benefits, campaigns, offers, calls for participation may indicate Advertisement



C. Deadline-action vs Event

- If attending is optional and the core point is the event itself → Event

- If the core point is that the student must register/apply/respond by a deadline → Deadline-action



D. Administrative vs Deadline-action

- If the email mainly describes a procedure/service/official notice → Administrative

- If it mainly pushes the student to complete an action by a date → Deadline-action



E. Course-exam vs Event

- If tied to a course, exam, quiz, instructor, course platform, or teaching logistics → Course-exam

- If it is a general seminar/talk/workshop/public event → Event



F. Advertisement vs Event

- Event is for a specific scheduled happening

- Advertisement is broader promotion/opportunity/initiative and not primarily the event occurrence itself



G. Priority must be judged from the student’s perspective.

Not from the sender’s perspective.



H. Use the body more than the subject when they conflict, but use both.



I. For auto-generated forum/course notifications:

- If the content is clearly about a course/exam/quiz/classroom instruction, label Course-exam even if auto-generated.



J. Confidence:

Provide a confidence score from 0.00 to 1.00 for:

- topic_confidence

- priority_confidence



Use lower confidence when:

- text is noisy, incomplete, or ambiguous

- multiple labels compete strongly

- the subject is generic and body is weak
